01

Start here

This file has one row per company per trading day: a date, a ticker symbol, four prices, an adjusted price, and how many shares changed hands. That's it. No earnings, no news, no company names even — just 005930.KS and a number.

That's simpler than it sounds, and also stricter than it sounds: it means every argument we make this semester has to be buildable out of price and volume alone. Keep that in the back of your mind as you read — we'll come back to exactly what that does and doesn't let us claim in section 11.

02

Reading one row

Take a single row: 2020-03-16, TSLA, 31.30, 32.99, 29.48, 29.67, 29.67, 307342500. Here's what each field means.

Ticker is the company's trading symbol. A suffix after a dot tells you which exchange it trades on — and that turns out to matter a lot (section 04):

.KS → Korea (KRW) .HK → Hong Kong (HKD) .SW → Switzerland (CHF) .PA → Paris (EUR) .SR → Saudi Arabia (SAR) no suffix → US listing (USD)

A trading day isn't one price, it's thousands of trades happening continuously for hours. Open, High, Low, Close compress that whole day into four numbers: the first trade, the highest trade, the lowest trade, and the last trade. Volume is simply how many shares changed hands that day.

Anatomy of one real trading dayTSLA · March 16, 2020 · the week the market fell fastest during the COVID crash

Tesla opened the day at $31.30, briefly traded as high as $32.99 and as low as $29.48, then closed at $29.67 — down for the day. 307.3 million shares traded, far more than a calm day. One candle, four numbers, one volume bar.

03

Close vs. Adjusted Close

Two things happen to a stock's price that have nothing to do with the company getting more or less valuable: it can split (each share becomes several, each worth proportionally less) or it can pay a dividend (cash leaves the company and goes to shareholders, so the share is worth slightly less right after). Adj Close exists to undo both effects so you can measure real growth over time.

Here's the part most tutorials get wrong, and that we confirmed by checking real rows in this exact file:

What the public record saysAAPL, before its 4-for-1 split

Apple split its stock 4-for-1 on August 31, 2020. On August 28, 2020, the day before, its publicly reported closing price was roughly $499.

A truly "raw, as-traded" feed would show ~$499 for that date, forever — that's what actually printed on the tape that day.

What this file shows for the same dayRow: 2020-08-28, AAPL

Close = 124.81   Adj Close = 121.17

Both columns already reflect the split that hadn't happened yet on that date. This file rewrites the entire Close history every time a split occurs — not just Adj Close.

What this means for us

Don't assume Close is "the raw traded price" the way it might be elsewhere — here, it already isn't, for splits. The gap that's left over between Close and Adj Close (that 3% above) is dividends only. Rule of thumb: use Adj Close whenever you're computing a return or a growth rate; use Close only when you need the literal price someone would have paid on a given day.

Why the first one lies

AAPL's last close in the file is $264.58. Samsung's is ₩190,100. Same day, same column, incomparable units — on a shared axis, AAPL is a flat line pinned near zero. Whenever we compare tickers across the currency groups in section 02, we index each to 100 at a shared start date (or use percentage returns) instead of plotting raw price.

05

Returns & volatility

A return is just a percentage change: if a stock goes from $100 to $105, that's a 5% return. Volatility measures how much those returns bounce around — not how much money is lost, a common mix-up. A stock can be volatile and still end up way ahead; it just gets there on a much rougher path.

A steady mover vs. a jumpy oneKO and NVDA, indexed to 100 at 2006-01, ten-year window

KO — Coca-ColaNVDA — Nvidia

Same starting point, same ten years. KO's path is a gentle, fairly predictable climb. NVDA's covers the same ground but whipsaws the whole way — measured as the standard deviation of monthly returns across the full 20-year file, NVDA's typical monthly swing (13.6%) is about three times the size of KO's (4.6%). That's what "volatile" actually means, numerically.

A warning about "long term"

Stretch this same comparison to the full 20-year file instead of just these ten years, and NVDA finishes about 550× its starting value against KO's roughly 7× — a gap so large the two lines can't even share a readable axis anymore. We're not cherry-picking to make a point; it's the opposite point. Change the end date and the entire story flips. That's exactly why a real hypothesis has to name a specific time window instead of saying "long term" — more on this in section 09.

06

Do markets move together?

Correlation is a single number from −1 to 1 describing whether two things tend to move up and down together. 1 means they move in perfect lockstep, 0 means no relationship, negative means they tend to move opposite each other. Below is the real correlation of monthly returns between five companies from five different exchanges, computed across the entire file.

Monthly-return correlation, 2006–2026Five companies, five exchanges, full history

Darker means more correlated. Even the strongest pair here — Apple and LVMH (Paris) at 0.46 — is far short of 1. Averaged over 20 years, these markets are only loosely connected to each other.

The seed of a real question

"Averaged over 20 years" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Does that looseness hold up during a crisis, or does it tighten? This page won't answer that — it's exactly the kind of question a real hypothesis is built from.

08

Five ways this data bites back

A quick-reference list — check this before trusting any chart that spans regions, eras, or tickers with different start dates.

01

Mixed currencies

Six currencies share one Close column. Index to 100 or use returns — never raw price across tickers. (Section 04)

02

Uneven start dates

Only 33 of 49 tickers span the full file. PLTR starts 2020-09-30, BABA 2014-09-19, META 2012-05-18, TSLA 2010-06-29. Rebase comparisons to a date every plotted ticker actually has.

03

Trading calendars don't line up

Full-history row counts differ by exchange — US 5,065 · Switzerland 5,070 · Paris 5,152 · Korea 4,972 · Hong Kong 4,964. Joining on date across regions leaves gaps.

04

Close already includes splits

The retroactive rewrite from section 03. What's left between Close and Adj Close is dividends only.

05

No fundamentals in this file

Only price and volume — no earnings, no revenue, no news. We can talk about co-movement and timing, never about why. (Section 11)

09

What is a hypothesis

In everyday speech, "hypothesis" just means a guess. For this assignment it means something narrower: a specific, falsifiable claim about why the data looks the way it does — something the data could actually embarrass you on, not a fact everyone already agrees with.

Weak — a fact, not a hypothesis

"Tech stocks went up a lot."

True, obvious, nobody disagrees. One groupby, one chart, nothing left to say.

Strong — an actual hypothesis

"The post-2023 rally is a narrow AI-infrastructure story, not a broad tech one — chipmakers detached from the rest of large-cap tech."

Names a mechanism, a time window, a group split. It implies a comparison — and it could turn out to be wrong.

The four-part test

01
Falsifiable

You can say, in advance, what the chart would look like if you're wrong.

02
Non-obvious

If the reader already believes it, there's nothing to show them.

03
Specific

Named tickers, a named group, a named window — not "tech" or "long term."

04
Answerable

Provable with price and volume alone — see section 11 for what that rules out.

10

A story, not a fact

One hypothesis in isolation is still just a finding — a single chart, then you're done. A pile of unrelated hypotheses is a report. What makes a story is one central claim, followed by a chain of sub-claims, where each one answers the objection the previous chart raises. Here's what that chain looks like in practice, using the AI-rally example above purely to illustrate the pattern:

Central claim
The rally is narrow AI-infrastructure, not broad tech.
Objection
Maybe all big tech did this.
Chart
Chipmakers separate visibly from the rest of large-cap tech.
Objection
Maybe chipmakers are always this volatile — nothing new here.
Chart
Run the same comparison through 2008 and 2020: they moved with the market then. Something changed.
Objection
Maybe it's just one company dragging the average.
Chart
Remove that one company. Does the pattern survive? (The honest answer might be "not entirely" — that's fine.)
The part nobody tells you

The most interesting slide in a project like this is usually the one where the hypothesis partly breaks. If a chart shows the effect is mostly one company, that's not a failure to hide — "the effect is narrower than it looks" is often the better finding. Go looking for the crack instead of around it.

11

What you can (and can't) prove with this file

You can credibly claim

  • Co-movement and correlation between companies or regions
  • Relative performance — who outran whom, and over what window
  • Volatility and drawdowns — how rough the ride was
  • The timing of when behavior changed
  • Dispersion within a group — how much the members differ

You cannot claim

  • That anything caused anything — there's no earnings or news data here
  • Anything about revenue, valuation, or company quality
  • Anything about management decisions or strategy
  • Anything about what happens next

Timing and correlation breaks are your strongest evidence. When a chart shows something changed on a specific date, let the reader connect that to what they already know happened — don't assert the cause yourself.
